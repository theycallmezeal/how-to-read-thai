Lesson 14

It's been a while since we've thought about consonants. We've gotten a lot of practice with the really common ones, and we've even met two of the rare Sanskrit / Pali ones, ฎ and ฏ. It's time to meet the rest of them!

I've grouped these four together because they all look annoyingly similar:

Letter Romanization Class
ฌ ch low
ญ y low
ณ n low
ฒ t low

Oh yeah. They're super annoying.

The right part of ณ kind of looks like the letter it sounds like, น. The left part of ฒ kind of looks like ต, and I guess t and dt sound close.

ญ has that little loopy thing on the bottom. This letter also has a special rule: when you write vowels under it - อุ or อู, that loopy part disappears: ญุ ญู. Good luck finding a word that has these combinations, though.

ณ appears in some common words, including a very important one: คุณ kun, which means "you". ญ also appears in a few common words.

ฌ and ฒ are, like, actually pretty useless. I had to look really hard to find words that had these two letters. Speaking of which, let's try some words:

ฌาน chaam dhyana, a Buddhist word that you might know by its Japanese version, zen
ญี่ปุ่น yîi-bpùn Japan
ฌาปน chaa-bpà-ná funeral
เฌอ chəə an archaic word for "wood" that's the go-to "word that starts with ฌ" (link)
ผู้เฒ่า pûu-tâo elder
ญาณี yaa-nii wise person
ญวน yuuan Vietnam
คุณ kun you
โบราณ boo-raan ancient

There you have it. Four super-rare letters that look really similar to each other. If you forget about them after reading these words, I won't blame you. (Well, remember the word คุณ, that's pretty important.)

Other rules

Dead syllables end in one short vowel or p t k. All other syllables are live.

Syllables with no written vowels have inherent vowels: a for one letter and o for two.

บ ค ก sound like p t k at the end of a syllable.
