Lesson 15

We're rounding the corner! We've covered all the fundamentals, and in the last quarter of our course, we're going to go over a lot of "side rules" that kind of add on to the strong foundation we've built.

Today we're going to learn two very important - and common - rules. They both work in similar ways, but one gets used all the time, and the other is only used in four words - really. (At least they're common words.) But first up, remember a few things about consonant classes:

What kind of sounds come in high / low pairs? Aspirated stops, or puffy sounds (p t ch k), and fricatives, or blowy sounds (f s h).
What kind of sound is always low-class? The sonorants, or sing-y sounds (m n ng l r y w).

Isn't it weird that the sonorants / sing-y sounds don't also have high counterparts? Thing is - they actually do.

Fortunately, though, we're not going to learn a bunch of letters. Instead, a simple rule: adding an ห to one of the "sing-y" letters turns it from a low-class to a high-class consonant. (If you're using my weird system for tones, adding an ห turns it from a "normal" consonant to a "weird" consonant!) The "sing-y" letters, as a reminder, are ม น ณ ง ล ร ย ญ ว. (There's one more we haven't met, ฬ.)

Let's look at all the things this affects. When I see หน, I know the ห that makes it high-class, and high-class consonants have names with rising tone, so I see หน and I think nɔ̌ɔ, which gets me into a high-class kind of mood.

In a live syllable, หนา nǎa "thick" has a rising tone, just like its name, nɔ̌ɔ.

In a dead syllable, หนัก nàk "heavy" has a low tone. (Note that we don't have to care about vowel length here, even though there's a น!)

With tone mark number one, หนึ่ง nʉ̀ng "one" has a low tone.

And with tone mark number two, หน้า nâa "in front of" has a falling tone.

Check out the spelling. For the first time, we're putting two consonants at the start of a syllable. When we do this, and then we use things that go on top of consonants, like vowels or tone marks, we put them on top of the *second* consonant. We write หน้า, not ห้นา.

But other than that, the two letters stick together, no matter what. So when we use things that go to the left of consonants, like ไอ, we put them to the left of both consonants: ไหม.

And when we use things that go *around* consonants, like เอา, we put them around both consonants: เหลา.

Here's a challenge. Try combining the consonant หน with the vowel เอือ. Where do all the parts go? How do you pronounce the resulting syllable? เหนือ nʉ̌ʉa north

Let's practice the ห rule with these words. It's very common! There are a variety of syllable types in there. The three words at the top there all sound very similar, but mean different things!

ไหม mǎi silk
ใหม่ mài new or again
ไหม้ mâi to burn
หยาบ yàap coarse
หมวก mùuak anything you wear on your head
หมด mòt empty or gone
หนี nǐi to escape
หรือ rʉ̌ʉ or
หนุ่ม nùm young man
หลัง lǎng behind
กำหนด gam-nòt appointment
ผู้หญิง pûu-yǐng woman
เหมือน mʉ̌ʉan same
ระหว่าง rá-wàang between
จังหวัด jang-wàt province
หน้าที่ nâa-tîi duty or responsibility

Sometimes the combos with ห act as one single high-class consonant, as we've been discussing. But sometimes they're just two letters that are next to each other! Check out the word เหลา. You can read it as one syllable, lǎo, with หล acting as a single thing. But you can also read it like two syllables: เห-ลา hěelaa, which is also a word that shares the same spelling.

Here's another example: พาหนะ is พา-ห-นะ, paa-há-ná, even though the หน looks like it might go together. Isn't that frustrating? Fortunately, that's kind of rare. In practice, ห next to a "sing-y" consonant is almost always turning it into a high-class consonant.

Applying this rule with the ห is really working our knowledge of the tones! Let's learn the second rule now. It's much simpler.

There are four words that start with อย. The อ combines with the ย and the two act as a single consonant. You pronounce it like a regular ย, and - good news! - the tone is always low. Here are the four words, all of which are common:

อยู่ yùu to be located or to live
อย่าง yàang a variety of something, like or as, or use it with an adjective to make an adverb, like the English -ly
อย่า yàa "don't" in commands, like "don't do that!"
อยาก yàak to want

That's all four of them, and they're all very common. Notice that three of them have tone mark number one on them, but อยาก doesn't. That's just how you spell them!

(If you want to be very technical about it, adding the อ to the ย turns it into a mid-class consonant. If you look at the four words and pretend they start with a mid-class consonant, then they're all low-tone. I think it's a lot easier, though, to just know that อย always means low tone, since there are only four words.)

Other rules

Dead syllables end in one short vowel or p t k. All other syllables are live.

Syllables with no written vowels have inherent vowels: a for one letter and o for two.

บ ค ก sound like p t k at the end of a syllable.

ห plus a sonorant or sing-y letter makes it high-class.

Words starting with อย have low tone.

When two consonants combine at the start of a syllable, nothing goes between them and they always stay together. Things that are written on top get written on the second consonant.
