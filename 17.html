Lesson 17

We basically know all there is to know about vowels and tones. The rest of our time we'll spend working on consonants.

Today, we're focusing on consonants at the end of a syllable. We know almost all the consonants, but we've only seen most of them at the start of a syllable.

The only ones we've seen at the end of a syllable are ม น ง บ ด ก. What sounds do they make at the end of a syllable? m n ng p t k.

Most other consonants can appear at the ends of syllables, too - but it's important to recognize that only those six consonant sounds can be at the end of a syllable.

So what about the other consonants? They change their sound into whichever of the six ending sounds is most like them.

For the sounds b bp p the ending sound most like them is p. So all the letters that normally make those sounds, บ ป ผ พ ภ, sound like a p at the end of a syllable.

For the sounds g and k, the ending sound most like them is k, so all the g and k letters, ก ข ค, sound like a k at the end of a syllable.

The next group is big: for the sounds d dt t j ch s, the ending sound most like them is t, so the many, many letters that make those sounds, ด ฎ ต ฏ ฒ ถ ท จ ฉ ฌ ช ษ ส ซ, all sound like a t at the end of a syllable.

The sounds r and l sound most like the ending sound n, so they sound like an n at the end of a word.

The last one is weird: remember the annoyingly rare letter ญ? It sounds like a y at the start of a word, but it sounds like an n at the end of a word. It's like this because ญ corresponds to a "ny" sound in Sanskrit and Pali, and that's how the sound was adapted into Thai pronunciation.

However, although the much more common ย also sounds like a y at the start of a word, if you see it at the end of a word, it's part of a vowel combination, or it's a vowel "bonus".

Letters that make the rest of the sounds - ฝ and ฟ for f, ห and ฮ for h, and the silent letter อ, never appear at the end of a word.

Let's sum up:

"Normal sound" Ending sound
b bp p p
d dt t j ch s t
g k k
r l n
ญ but not ย n

It's not super common to see most consonants at the ends of syllables. In native Thai vocabulary, you'll usually only see ม น ง บ ด ก, so if you see a weird consonant at the end of a syllable, it's probably preserving the spelling from Sanskrit, Pali, or English, which allow way more sounds in syllable ends than Thai does.

Try out the full variety of ending consonants with these words. I encourage you to figure out the sound by vibing with it instead of memorizing. Just remember to turn the letter into the ending sound that it's closest to.

Other rules

Dead syllables end in one short vowel or p t k. All other syllables are live.

Syllables with no written vowels have inherent vowels: a for one letter and o for two.

The only permitted ending sounds are m n ng p t k. Consonants at the end of a syllable change to the closest-sounding ending sound:

"Normal sound" Ending sound
b bp p p
d dt t j ch s t
g k k
r l n
ญ but not ย n

ห plus a sonorant or sing-y letter makes it high-class.

Words starting with อย have low tone.

When two consonants combine at the start of a syllable, nothing goes between them and they always stay together. Things that are written on top or below get written on the second consonant.
