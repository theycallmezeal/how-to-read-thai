Lesson 13

How are you doing with tone mark number one? It can be a little tricky to keep the rules straight once you mix it in with the toneless syllables.

Let's bring in tone mark number two. It's called ไม้โท máai too. โท is the Sanskrit number two, and tone mark number two looks kinda like a little number two: อ้. There's one in the name of ไม้โท!

Tone mark number two works a lot like tone mark number one. When you see it, all that matters is the class of the first consonant. No worrying about live or dead, long or short, none of that:

If the syllable starts with a low-class consonant, the tone is high.

If the syllable starts with a mid-class or high-class consonant, the tone is falling.

Let's look at the word ไม้โท máai too. You might recall I mentioned in lesson 11 that this word has an irregular spelling: ไม้ is máai with a long vowel even though it looks like it has a short vowel with ไอ. (More on irregular spellings here.) But the tone is exactly what we expect: because the syllable starts with a low-class consonant, the tone is high!

Let's put tone mark number two in our trusty table:

First consonant Tone
live dead อ่ อ้
short long
low-class mid high falling falling high
mid-class mid low low falling
high-class rising low low falling

Let's think about tone in some new ways. You might find these ways easier or harder - do what you find most natural.

With the live syllables - the "nice" case - we can just use the tone that we use to say the first letter. If the first letter is sɔ̌ɔ, for example, we just reuse that rising tone.

In all other cases - the dead syllables and the syllables with tone marks - the mid and high-class consonants always behave the same! So I group the mids and highs together into the "weird" consonants.

  • The high-class consonants are weird because you say their names with rising tone - they just feel different when you say them.
  • The mid-class unaspirated stops, the "touchy" sounds, are weird because they're the only ones that get their own category.
  • And the mid-class oddball อ is weird because nobody else is like it!

Compared to those sounds, the low-class consonants feel more "default" or "normal" to me.

With all that said, my personal version of the tone table looks more like:

First consonant Tone
live dead อ่ อ้
"normal" consonants
(low-class)		 same tone as
letter name		 short vowel: high
long vowel: falling		 falling high
"weird" consonants
(mid and high-class)		 low low falling

Do you find this simpler to think about? I do. You might not, it's all good! With tone marks one and two under our belts, we're now over a major hump: we've learned basically all the major tone rules. In the next few lessons, we'll learn about a few other fiddly bits that affect tone, but details schmetails. The worst is behind us. That's tone! We did tone!

Other rules

Dead syllables end in one short vowel or p t k. All other syllables are live.

Syllables with no written vowels have inherent vowels: a for one letter and o for two.

บ ค ก sound like p t k at the end of a syllable.
