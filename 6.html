Lesson 6

Lesson 5 was a doozy! Let's have a more easygoing lesson today. Take this opportunity to review the new letters from lesson 5, and most importantly, the tone rule.

All we're doing today is five consonants. Here are two of the new ones, both of which look annoyingly close to ถ:

Letter Romanization Class
ภ p low
ก g mid

ภ and พ make the same sound and are in the same class! What's the difference, then? Spelling. You just have to know which words use which letter.

Here are some words that use the two new letters:

ภาคี paa-kii member
กา gaa crow (the bird)
ภู puu mound
กาง gaang to spread out

Here's another new letter:

Letter Romanization Class
ษ s high
This is a high-class letter s. What other high-class letter s have we already met? What's the low-class letter s again? ส is high-class and ซ is low-class!

This letter isn't as common as the high-class s we've already met, but we can still have some fun with it. Remember what being high-class does to the tone:

ภาษา paa-sǎa language
ภาษี paa-sǐi tax

Now, the last two letters. They're another one of our low/high-class pairs:

Letter Romanization Class
ฟ f low
ฝ f high

These letters are actually based on พ and ผ, another low and high pair - the f letters are just taller on the right. Try them out in these words, being mindful of what the two different classes mean for tone:

ฝา fǎa lid
ไฟ fai fire

Let's bring it home with this very long and important word. There's a spelling quirk in there, but try sounding this out:

ภาษาไทย paa-sǎa tai the Thai language

Sounding out the word ไทย, you hopefully figured out that it goes t-ai-y. When you say that out loud, the y sound kind of disappears and leaves just tai.

Why not just write ไท? Spelling. A few words with ไอ just do that - they have an extra ย at the end that kind of "blends in". We'll learn more about ย at the end of a word later in the course.

Practice breaking this up into syllables! Make sure you recognize that ไทย is all one syllable.

What are the syllables of ภาษาไทย? ภา-ษา-ไทย. paa-saa tai.

Believe it or not, that's all the consonants! Kind of. We've covered all the consonants that are common. As we'll see in future lessons, the rest of the consonants are kind of rare. In our next few lessons, we're about to tackle some of the most complex parts of Thai writing, and the consonants we have in our arsenal will be more than plenty to learn them.
