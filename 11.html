Lesson 11

Alright, time to put on our big kid pants. The tone marks in Thai are everywhere, and we've gone as far as we can in our journey reading Thai without learning about them. Time to take them head on.

The good news is that the tone marks make figuring out tone a lot simpler than we're used to. When you have a tone mark, all that matters is the class of the first consonant. That's it!

Without tone marks, we have to think about live or dead syllables and long or short vowels. There's none of that with tone marks. Isn't that nice?

Here's what our first tone mark looks like: อ่. It's called ไม้เอก máai-èek. (You can read the word เอก! Don't worry about the word ไม้. It has an irregular spelling - and a tone mark we haven't met yet!)

ไม้ means "mark", and เอก comes from the Sanskrit word for "one". So this is literally "mark number one", and sure enough, it looks like a little number one.

When you see ไม้เอก, tone mark number one, here's what it means for tone:

If the syllable starts with a low-class consonant, the tone is falling.

If the syllable starts with a mid-class or high-class consonant, the tone is low.

Here's an example word: สี่ทุ่ม. I see two syllables in there: สี่ and ทุ่ม.

สี่ starts with a high-class consonant. Because it's high-class, and because we have tone mark number one, this is a low-tone syllable: sìi.

ทุ่ม starts with a low-class consonant. Low-class consonant plus tone mark number one means falling tone: tûm.

Putting it together, สี่ทุ่ม is sìi tûm, meaning 10 p.m.

Let's write down what we've learned about tone mark number one into our table. If it helps, visualize where you are on the table whenever you need to figure out tone:

First consonant Tone
live dead อ่
short long
low-class mid high falling falling
mid-class mid low low
high-class rising low low

Notice that if the syllable starts with a low-class consonant, there are two ways to get a falling tone: with a dead syllable and a long vowel, or with tone mark number one.

Also, if the syllable starts with a mid- or high-class consonant, now we have two ways to get a low tone: either with a closed syllable or with tone mark number one.

That's it! That's all we're learning today. This is an important rule, and it's one that comes up all the dang time. It can be challenging to work this one in with the other tone rules. So here's a lot of practice words. Good luck!

Tone rules

First consonant Tone
live dead อ่
short long
low-class mid high falling falling
mid-class mid low low
high-class rising low low

Other rules

Dead syllables end in one short vowel or p t k. All other syllables are live.

Syllables with no written vowels have inherent vowels: a for one letter and o for two.

บ ค ก sound like p t k at the end of a syllable.
