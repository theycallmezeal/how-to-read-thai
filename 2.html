Lesson 2

We're about to learn a lot more consonants, which means we're going to have to get organized.

First, let's think about all the sounds in Thai:

p t ch k
f s h
bp dt g b d j
m n ng l r y w

It will help to break these up into groups:

p t ch k
These are aspirated stops, or puffy sounds. When you say them, you make a little puff of air - put your hand in front of your mouth to see.
f s h
These are fricatives, or blowy sounds. To make them, you have to blow outwards. Try it!
bp dt g b d j
These are unaspirated stops, or touchy sounds. To make them, you touch different parts of your mouth together and release.
m n ng l r y w
These are sonorants, or sing-y sounds. You can hold them for a long time, and it's almost like you "sing" them for as long as you hold them.
m n ng
These three sing-y sounds are also nasals, or nose-y sounds. You actually release air through your nose when you "sing" them. Try it!

So what's the big deal? The reason you should know these is because they help you determine the class of a consonant.

Thai consonant letters are broken up into three classes - low, mid, and high. (Important: the names of the classes have nothing to do with the names of the tones we'll eventually learn.) What class a consonant is in depends on what kind of sound it makes:

  • Consonants that make aspirated or puffy sounds, or fricatives or blowy sounds, can be either low-class or high-class. (There are always at least two letters for these sounds: one low, one high.)
  • Consonants that make stops or touchy sounds are mid-class.
  • Consonants that make sonorants or sing-y sounds are low-class.

Let's practice!

What consonant classes are all these consonants in, and why? ม น ง ย ร They're all low-class because they're sonorants or sing-y sounds.
What consonant classes could this consonant be in, and why? ช This is an aspirated stop or puffy sound, so it could be either low or high-class. (For the linguists: yeah, yeah, it's an affricate, but it phonologically patterns like a stop in Thai.)

I'll tell you now that ช is low-class :) As promised, it has its high-class buddy that also makes the ch sound. It looks like ฉ, if you're curious. For now, just remember which is which. All of our consonants are actually going to be low or mid class for now. We'll start working with high-class consonants in unit 5.

And why do we care about consonant classes? Because they matter for tone! This may seem tedious now, but you should get good at remembering what classes all your consonants are in, because you'll need them for tone, and you need tone to be understood.
