Lesson 16

Today we're thinking about vowels again. I think it's a good time to take inventory of the vowels we know:

อั/อะ อา ออ อว/อัว a aa ɔɔ uua
อิ อี อึ อื/อือ i ii ʉ ʉʉ
อุ อู u uu
ไอ ใอ โอ ai ai oo
เอ แอ เอา เอิ/เออ ee ɛɛ ao əə
เอีย เอือ iia ʉʉa

Make sure you can read all of these, because we'll be adding on to them in this lesson.

Remember how we realized we knew how to write all of our long vowel sounds, but none of the short ones? It's time to fix that. Meet the vowel shorteners:

อ็ อะ

Here's two words you know how to read:

เยน yeen Japanese yen
แล lɛɛ to see or look

Now, try shortening the vowel once you add อ็ or อะ:

เย็น yen evening or cold
และ lɛ́ and

As always, the difference between long and short in Thai is important - these are different words!

The tones in แล and และ are different! Why? Although both words start with low-class consonants, แล is a live syllable, so it gets mid tone, while และ is a dead syllable that gets high tone.
We added a vowel shortener to turn เยน into เย็น. Why didn't the tone change this time? Both are live syllables that start with low-class consonants, so both are mid tone.
Reminder: what makes a syllable live or dead? Dead syllables end in a single short vowel or p t k. Everything else is live.

Why are there two vowel shorteners? อ็ is used when the syllable ends in a consonant, and อะ is used when it doesn't.

A spelling note: even though อ็ shortens the vowel, you write it on the first consonant of the syllable.

Remember, อะ is also used on its own as a short a, so make sure to recognize when it's paired with another vowel and when it's on its own!

Normally, you use อ็ อะ with long vowels. There's one exception, though: เอาะ. เอา on its own is ao, but when you shorten it, you get เอาะ ɔ, which kinda sorta sounds like a smooshed-together ao, I guess.

The rest of the combinations with อ็ and อะ play nice. Don't memorize these - you can figure them out on the fly:

  • เอะ/เอ็ e
  • แอะ/แอ็ ɛ
  • โอะ o
  • อ็อ ɔ
  • เออะ ə
  • เอียะ ia
  • เอือะ ʉa
  • อัวะ ua

อ็อ and เอาะ make the same sound, ɔ! เอาะ is way more common. (Disappointing - it's the harder one to remember.)

There are two more vowel "bonuses" you can add on to create even more sounds: ย and ว.

If you add ย onto the end of an existing vowel, you add an i sound to the end:

ชา chaa tea
ชาย chaai male

If you add ว, you add an o sound - well, it sounds more like a "w", but it's spelled o in the system we're using:

ข้า kâa servant
ข้าว kâao rice

The letters ย and ว do a lot of different things!

ย can be:

  • the consonant y
  • part of the combination เอีย iia
  • a vowel "bonus" to add an i

ว can be:

  • the consonant w
  • its own vowel อัว/อว uua
  • a vowel "bonus" to add an o sound

This can make reading ambiguous sometimes, but you can usually vibe with it and make a good guess.

There is, of course, another special case: เอย looks like เอ plus ย, so you'd think it sounds like eei, but that sound doesn't exist in Thai! เอย actually sounds like əəi.

The rest of the pairings with ย and ว play nice, though. Again, don't memorize these; you can figure them out on the fly:

  • อัย ai
  • ไอย ai
  • อาย aai
  • ออย ɔɔi
  • โอย ooi
  • อุย ui
  • เอือย ʉʉai
  • อาว aao
  • แอว ɛɛo
  • เอว eeo
  • เอียว iiao
  • อิว iu (This one is pronounced as you'd expect - the system of English letters we're using just writes iu instead of io.)

There are actually a lot of ways to write the sound ai with a short a: ไอ ใอ อัย ไอย. And there's a different way to write aai with a long a: อาย.

Also remember that short ao is เอา (its own special combination), but long aao is written อาว (with the "building blocks" อา and ว next to each other).

You can combine them with the vowel shorteners, too!

  • อ็อย ɔi
  • แอ็ว ɛo
  • เอ็ว eo

All together now: let's practice our new vowel bonuses with these words. Remember, even though our focus today was vowels, we're still practicing tone. I see plenty of tone marks in there. A syllable ending in a single short vowel is dead - keep that in mind when reading the vowel shorteners!

Let's sum up. With these four vowel "bonuses" and two exceptions, we now know everything there is to know about the vowels!

อ็/อะ shorten vowels.

ย adds an i sound and ว adds an o sound.

Exceptions: เอาะ ɔ and เอย əəi.

Other rules

Dead syllables end in one short vowel or p t k. All other syllables are live.

Syllables with no written vowels have inherent vowels: a for one letter and o for two.

บ ค ก sound like p t k at the end of a syllable.

ห plus a sonorant or sing-y letter makes it high-class.

Words starting with อย have low tone.

When two consonants combine at the start of a syllable, nothing goes between them and they always stay together. Things that are written on top get written on the second consonant.
