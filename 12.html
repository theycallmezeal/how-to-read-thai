Lesson 12

We've met almost all our vowels! I'm itching to learn the last few and complete our vowel picture.

Here's our first set:

Letter Romanization Length
อึ ʉ short
อื ʉʉ long
อือ ʉʉ long

We have a short and long version, and they both look kind of similar. The short vowel, อึ, has a little circle attached to it. The long vowel, อื, has two little notches. Also note that the long vowel (and only the long vowel) has two versions: อื, when there's a consonant at the end of the syllable, and อือ, when there isn't. That อ might look like it's a separate letter sometimes, so be careful!

Let's compare these to the short i อิ and long ii อี we met previously. The short i has nothing on it - it's just the little half-moon shape. The long ii has just one notch on it. These all look similar!

Let's practice the new ʉ sound in its short and long versions:

คืน kʉʉn night
สืบ sʉ̀ʉp to investigate
ซื่อ sʉ̂ʉ honest
ลืม lʉʉm to forget
มือ mʉʉ hand
ยืน yʉʉn to stand
นับถือ náp-tʉ̌ʉ to respect
คือ kʉʉ to be
ซึ่ง sʉ̂ng "that", like "the language that I speak"
นึก nʉ́k to think
ชื่อ chʉ̂ʉ name (you got a sneak preview of this word in lesson 1!)
ฝึกหัด fʉ̀k-hàt to drill or practice
จึง jʉng so / therefore
ดื่ม dʉ̀ʉm to drink
ตึก dtʉ̀k building
ของที่ระลึก kɔ̌ɔng tîi rá-lʉ́k souvenir - literally "thing (ของ) to (ที่) remember (ระลึก)"

We've now met all of our vowel sounds, so let's take an inventory and do a pronunciation check.

There are nine vowel sounds in Thai. (Some of the vowel letters you've learned, like เอา, represent diphthongs, or two vowel sounds put together.)

These vowel sounds can all be short or long, and you now know how to read the long versions of all nine vowels. Say them out loud, taking note that all of them are different sounds:

อี อื/อือ อู
เอ เอิ/เออ โอ
แอ อา ออ ii ʉʉ uu
ee əə oo
ɛɛ aa ɔɔ

(The linguists will know why I arranged them like that!)

We've also met a few short vowels. These aren't in any order. Read them, and match them up to their corresponding long vowels.

อั/อะ a, the short version of อา
อิ i, the short version of อี
อึ ʉ, the short version of อื/อือ
อุ u, the short version of อู

You might be wondering: if all nine vowels come in short and long versions, where are the rest of the short vowels? To write them, we'll need "bonus" symbols that will be introduced in a future lesson.

Just two more "basic" vowels to go! I saved my favorites for last. These are really fun because they actually have three parts: to the left, on top, and to the right of the consonant:

Letter Romanization Length
เอีย iia long ii, short a
เอือ ʉʉa long ʉʉ, short a

What's that I see? It's a ย! It's part of the three-part set that makes up เอีย. Sometimes you might be tempted to look at it like the regular letter ย, so be careful! It's the same deal with that อ at the end of เอือ.

Let's try some words with these new vowels. Just like อว, they count as long vowels for the purposes of tone:

เสือ sʉ̌ʉa tiger
เกือบ gʉ̀ʉap almost
เลือก lʉ̂ʉak to choose
เสียง sǐiang a sound or noise
เรือ rʉʉa boat
เมื่อไร mʉ̂ʉa-rai when
เชื่อ chʉ̂ʉa to believe
นักเรียน nák-riian student
ละเอียด lá-ìiat detailed
เสียใจ sǐia-jai to be sorry or to regret
เงียบ ngîiap silent / quiet
เมีย miia a very informal word for "wife"

There's one more vowel, and this one's a weird one:

Letter Romanization Length
อํา am short

Bet you weren't expecting to see a consonant in there too. That's right, this one is a short a and an m, and it behaves just the same as if you wrote อัม. Because it ends in an m sound, syllables with อํา are live. Try these words out:

รำ ram to dance
ตำ dtam to hit
ทำงาน tam ngaan to work
นำ nam to lead
กำไร gam-rai profit
บำรุง bam-rung to care for
ลำบาก lam-bàak difficult
สำนัก sǎm-nák office

And that's it! Those are all the "basic" vowels. There are, of course, more vowel sounds in Thai, but we'll learn to write them in a later lesson by using the "basic" vowels and adding things to them.

Tone rules

First consonant Tone
live dead อ่
short long
low-class mid high falling falling
mid-class mid low low
high-class rising low low

Other rules

Dead syllables end in one short vowel or p t k. All other syllables are live.

Syllables with no written vowels have inherent vowels: a for one letter and o for two.

บ ค ก sound like p t k at the end of a syllable.
