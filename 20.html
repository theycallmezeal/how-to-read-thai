Lesson 20

Last lesson! This rule is one of the hardest, and it's kind of unpredictable, so we've saved it for last. Last lesson, we looked at "normal" consonant clusters - the ones that behave.

However, there's another kind of consonant cluster that I'm calling the "hidden-a" cluster. I'll show you with an example: สมัย sàmǎi.

At first glance, it looks like สมัย is a word we can read. What's weird about the pronunciation, sàmǎi? The tones are unexpected. This word looks like ส-มัย sà-mai. But the second syllable is mǎi with a rising tone instead.

So something's going on with that สม cluster. Here's how to read it:

  • I look at the first consonant, ส. I imagine I add a short อะ to it and make สะ sà.
  • I look at the class of the first consonant. ส is high-class.
  • I read the rest of the syllable as normal, except I pretend that it starts with the class of the first consonant. So I read มัย, but when I figure out the tone, I pretend that the syllable starts with a high-class consonant. High-class consonant, live syllable, rising tone, so this is mǎi.
  • I put the two parts together: sàmǎi.

To recap - hidden-a clusters become two syllables. The first syllable is the first consonant plus อะ. The second syllable "borrows" its starting consonant class from the first consonant. (There is, of course, a very rare exception to this that I talk about in the epilogue.)

Usually, if you see a high-class consonant that's followed by a sonorant or "sing-y" sound, it's probably a hidden-a cluster. (Remember, the sonorants are m n ng r l y w.) Hidden-a clusters starting with mid-class consonants are less common, and hidden-a clusters starting with low-class consonants are very rare.

This rule is annoying because there's nothing that tells you when it applies. For example, check out the word สมณะ. You might be tempted to say สม is a hidden-a cluster and get sàmàná. But nope! Instead of being a cluster, this สม is just two separate syllables: ส-ม. The word is sàmáná.

Here's another example. The word ปลัด has a hidden-a cluster: bpàlàt. But there are plenty of words where ปล is just a "regular" cluster instead: ปลูก, ปลา, and so on.

I had to look pretty hard for those examples, though. In the real world, here's three things about words with hidden-a clusters:

  1. High-class consonant plus sonorant / "sing-y" sound usually equals hidden-a cluster. Especially ส. Hidden-a clusters love to start with ส.
  2. Because high and mid-class consonants plus อะ create low-tone syllables, the first syllable of a hidden-a cluster will almost always be low.
  3. In practice, you can kind of fudge the pronunciation. See how สมัย kind of looks like it's one syllable, smǎi? Especially when people talk quickly, this word can actually sound kind of like a one-syllable s'mǎi.

So - honestly - don't worry too much about this rule. It comes up in some common vocabulary, but once you know a word, you'll know what it sounds like, and you won't have to think through the rule every time.

Why did this weird rule come about? Most of the words with this rule come from Khmer, which is spoken in Cambodia. Just like Sanskrit and Pali, Thai has a lot of Khmer words, and Khmer has a lot of consonant clusters that Thai doesn't let you say, so Thai just smooshed a little a sound in there.

Try these words out. There's just a few of them for practice, and I promise each of them contains a hidden-a cluster. Because in the real world there's no telling when this rule applies, I really think that when you learn new vocabulary you should just learn these cases as they come.

Other rules

Dead syllables end in one short vowel or p t k. All other syllables are live.

Syllables with no written vowels have inherent vowels: a for one letter and o for two.

The only permitted ending sounds are m n ng p t k. Consonants at the end of a syllable change to the closest-sounding ending sound:

"Normal sound" Ending sound
b bp p p
d dt t j ch s t
g k k
r l n
ญ but not ย n

ห plus a sonorant or sing-y letter makes it high-class.

Words starting with อย have low tone.

When two consonants combine at the start of a syllable, nothing goes between them and they always stay together. Things that are written on top or below get written on the second consonant.

"Hidden-a" clusters turn into two syllables. The first syllable is the first consonant plus อะ. The second syllable consists of everything else, except the consonant class used to determine tone is taken from the first consonant.
