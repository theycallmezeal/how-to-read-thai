Lesson 1

Let's start things off with two letters:

Letter Romanization
ม m
า aa

Note that we have a long aa. As you'll see later, long and short vowels are different!

Now read this word:

มาmaa come

Awesome! This word has a mid tone. It should be level and in the middle of your vocal range. We'll find out why it's mid tone later.

We can do a lot with this long aa. Here are some more consonants to play with: five more of 'em:

Letter Romanization
น n
ง ng
ช ch
ย y
ร r

ม and น look really similar. The "m" has the loopy part on the left, and the "n" has the loopy part on the right.

Let's practice our new letters. Try reading these words, all of which also have mid tone:

งาน ngaan work / job
นาม naam name (a formal word. The normal word meaning "name" is ชื่อ chʉ̂ʉ. You don't know how to read this yet, but do you recognize a letter?)
ชา chaa tea
งาม ngaam beautiful
ยา yaa medicine
นา naa rice paddy
ยาม yaam guard or period of time

Did you notice that ง ng can be at the start of a word? This can be kind of hard to pronounce. If you're having trouble, try using an English word with "ng", like "sing", to transition into the Thai "ng": try saying, sing-งาน. sing-ngaan. Practice getting into position with the English word, then hold it, pause, and say the Thai word.

Here's our longest word yet:

ราชา raa-chaa king

This word is two syllables! Both syllables, the รา raa and the ชา chaa, have mid tone.

Syllables are very important in Thai! Note that some syllables are consonant-vowel, like นา naa, while others are consonant-vowel-consonant, like ยาม yaam.

Let's review what we've learned! When you say the names of the consonants we've met so far, you add ɔɔ to the end of it and use a mid tone. ย is yɔɔ, for example.

The letters all have "official" names that incorporate a word that has that letter. ย is ย ยักษ์ yɔɔ yák, for example. You don't have to memorize the official names of all the letters, but many of them incorporate objects important to Thai culture. They're listed in the appendix if you're curious.
