Lesson 7

Big lesson today! We're going to talk about tone, vowels, and one of the biggest things to watch out for when you read Thai: live and dead syllables.

But let's start out with some review:

How do you read these vowels again? อา อี อู อา is aa, อี is ii, and อู is uu. They are all long vowels.

We've met a lot of long vowels so far. Let's meet our first short vowels:

Letter Romanization Length
อั a short
อะ a short
อิ i short
อุ u short

The long vowels อี อู look like their short versions, อิ อุ, with extra little lines. Especially when text is printed small, it can be hard to see the difference. Do your best!

อั and อะ make the same sound: a. You use อั when there is a consonant at the end of the syllable, and you use อะ if there isn't. A couple vowels have similar variations depending on whether there's a consonant ending their syllables or not.

Let's practice the new short vowels. Be careful about tone:

หัน hǎn to turn
ฟัน fan tooth
ขัง kǎng to keep something confined
วัน wan day
ขัน khǎn any kind of bowl or vessel
ฝัง fǎng to bury
ฟัง fang to listen
มัน man a pronoun used for animals
ชิง ching to snatch away or compete
ยุง yung mosquito
จัง jang very; so
อัน an a classifier for small objects
ยิง ying to shoot a weapon
สิน sǐn wealth
กิน gin to eat or drink

Now let's move on to the big thing I promised you we'd talk about: live and dead syllables. Every syllable in Thai can be called either "live" or "dead", and it's important to know which is which, because it affects tone!

So far, every single syllable we've read is live. What makes a syllable dead? Remember this:

A dead syllable ends in either one short vowel or a p, t, or k. All other syllables are live.

Before we meet our first dead syllable, let's backtrack through a few words we've met before. As I mentioned, everything we've read so far has been live. Let's make sure we know why:

  • โคม - this word ends in an m. That's not something a dead syllable can end in.
  • ไถ - this words ends in two short vowels: ai. To be dead, this would need only one short vowel.
  • กา - this word ends in a long vowel: aa. To be dead, this would need to end in a single short vowel.

So what's so special about dead syllables? Their tone changes!

Dead syllables starting with low-class consonants have high tone*.

Dead syllables starting with mid-class consonants have low tone.

Dead syllables starting with high-class consonants have low tone.

(*There's more to this rule, because of course there is, but we'll get there soon enough.)

Sit with that for a moment: this is very important. Now, to determine the tone of a syllable, you've got to look at both the first letter and what comes at the end - checking if it's live or dead, and using both to figure out the tone.

It's kind of a lot, especially to start out! I promise it gets easier with time. Let's read a few words with dead syllables.

ละ lá per
ดุ dù to scold
คะ ká a question particle used by women
กะ gà to measure
นะ ná a particle that kind of means "isn't it?"
จะ já a particle that kind of means "going to" or "could"
อะไร à-rai what

Make sure you recognize why each syllable is dead!

There is one live syllable mixed in with the above words to keep things interesting. Can you find it, say why it's live, and read it with the correct tone? อะไร à-rai has two syllables. อะ is a dead syllable that starts with a mid-class consonant, so it's low tone à. But ไร is a live syllable that starts with a low-class consonant, so it's mid tone rai.

This was a big day, so let's recap what we've learned:

  • short and long vowels
  • live and dead syllables
  • determining the tone of dead syllables
