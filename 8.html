Lesson 8

At the end of lesson 6, we said we were done learning all the common consonants. We also said the rest of the consonants were super rare. Just because they're rare doesn't mean they're cool, though - while we'll be learning most of them near the tail end of the course, I want to give you a sneak preview of two of the cooler-looking rare consonants:

Letter Romanization
ฎ d
ฏ dt

These two letters look really similar. ฏ has an extra little bumpy bit on the bottom part.

I didn't tell you what class they're in on purpose. Based on the sounds they make, what class are they? They're unaspirated stops or touchy sounds, so they're mid-class.
What other consonants do you know that make the same sounds and are in the same class? ด d and ต dt.

Just like ภ and พ, we now have more consonants that behave just like each other. It's a spelling thing: you just have to know which words use which letters.

Like I said, ฎ and ฏ are... really, really rare. They only appear in words borrowed from the Indian languages Sanskrit and Pali, which massively influenced the language as Thailand was influenced by Indian culture. Sanskrit and Pali have a lot of letters that represent sounds that Thai doesn't have. Although words with these letters changed to fit Thai pronunciation, people still wanted to preserve the spelling. So ฎ and ฏ are used when the original Sanskrit / Pali word used different d and t sounds that Thai doesn't have. We'll wait till later in the course to learn the rest of the Sanskrit / Pali letters!

Here are some words that use these letters. They're super uncommon. I had to look pretty hard to find words that fit. Remember to look out for consonant classes and live and dead syllables to figure out tone:

ฎีกา dii-gaa petition to the king (told you these words were rare!)
โฏฏะ dtoo-dtà very small
กุฏิ gù-dtì monk house
ภูฏาน puu-dtaan Bhutan

Let's move on. We're going to go from two very rare letters to a very common part of Thai writing: inherent or unwritten vowels.

We're used to syllables that have vowels in them. But sometimes, you get a syllable that has no vowel in it! How do you read it then? We have a rule:

If there's one letter, you add a short a to the end.

If there's two letters, you add a short o in between them.

We call these unwritten a's and o's inherent vowels.

Here are a few words with inherent vowels. Give them a go, being mindful of tone.

คน kon person
มหา má-hǎa grand or great
ผม pǒm a polite word for "I" used by men
นม nom milk
สม sǒm suitable
ดง dong jungle, or to put rice back over low heat after draining it so it can finish cooking (yes, really)
ชฎา chá-daa a type of fancy headpiece
ตุลาคม dtù-laa-kom October
ยาปฏิชีวนะ yaa-bpà-dtì-chii-wá-ná antibiotics (ยา means medicine, ปฏิ means against or anti, and ชีวนะ means life, like an organism.)

That last word was a whopping six syllables, our longest word yet!

Unfortunately, it's tricky when multiple syllables come next to each other. For example, how did you know นม was one syllable and not two? Answer: you couldn't. I had to tell you.

Let's make it harder: can you find the syllables in this word?

กรกฎาคม

It's tricky, right? You might have guessed กร-ก-ฎาค-ม or ก-รก-ฎา-คม or a whole host of other guesses. You can't tell just by looking at the word!

Unfortunately, you just have to know the word. More generally:

You cannot always determine the syllables, and therefore the pronunciation, of every word from the spelling alone.

There's one hint in this word: the vowel า. Vowels attach to consonants. So at least we know ฎ and า are part of the same syllable. But inherent vowels give us no help!

In the real world, you just kinda make educated guesses, learn how words are spelled as you go, and it's really not that bad. For this word, you just have to know that it breaks up like this:

ก-ร-ก-ฎา-คม gà-rá-gà-daa-kom July

That's right, after going on and on about how ฎ is rare, here it is in the very common word for a month. I see two different kinds of inherent vowels in this word. This one's extra hard because there are a few consonant classes and live and dead syllables. Go slow!

First consonant Tone
live dead

Other rules

Dead syllables end in one short vowel or p t k. All other syllables are live.

Syllables with no written vowels have inherent vowels: a for one letter and o for two.
