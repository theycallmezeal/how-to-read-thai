Epilogue

That's it! That's really, honestly, it. Everything we've learned will cover, like, 99% of Thai. And it's a lot! We know about:

  • all 42 consonants, even the really rare Sanskrit / Pali ones
  • consonant classes, and what kinds of sounds go in which classes
  • live and dead syllables
  • long and short vowels
  • tone marks
  • how to figure out tone by looking at consonant classes, live and dead syllables, long and short vowels, and tone marks
  • vowel "bonuses"
  • the ห rule and the four อย words
  • consonants at the end of a syllable
  • consonant clusters
  • those pesky hidden-a clusters

This epilogue is devoted to the 1% of words that don't follow these rules. Before I get into them, though, take a moment to be proud of yourself for how much you already can do. The rest of these exceptions? Honestly, who cares.

Please don't memorize any of these exceptions; they're rare enough that I didn't teach them in the main lessons. With the rock-solid foundation you've built reading the words that do behave, I think you'll find that you'll be able to just roll with the exceptions.

"Phonetic Thai" spellings

A lot of words are spelled so weird that in dictionaries you will see them written out phonetically with more "normal" spellings. For example, you'll see สมัย, with a hidden-a cluster, written สะ-ไหม, which is more obviously sà-mǎi.

This isn't a standardized system at all - mostly it's just common sense, but I write more about it in the appendix.

"Phonetic Thai" is helpful when dealing with the exceptional spellings below. You'll see it in dictionaries and maybe in your learning materials too.

Tone marks three and four

There are two more tone marks. They're pretty rare, and they only appear in syllables starting with mid-class consonants. They're called ไม้ตรี máai dtrii and ไม้จัตวา máai jàt-dtà-waa, using the Sanskrit words for three and four.

Tone mark three looks like this: อ๊, like tone mark two with an extra little squiggle. It makes everything high tone.

โต๊ะ dtó table
ซีอิ๊ว sii íu soy sauce
แก๊ส gɛ́ɛt gas

Tone mark four looks like a plus sign: อ๋. It makes everything rising tone.

เดี๋ยว dǐiao soon
กระเป๋า grà-bpǎo bag
ลูกเต๋า lûuk-dtǎo dice

Rare though they may be, you never have to worry about consonant class or anything with tone marks three and four. Number three is high and number four is rising, every time!

Tone is just weird

Sometimes the tone rules will lie to you, and a word just has a weird tone for no reason.

ประโยค (ประ-โหยก) bprà-yòok sentence

In particular, a few common words will look like they're rising tone, but different people will pronounce them with rising or high tone.

ฉัน chǎn / chán I (said by women)
เขา kǎo / káo (s)he
ไหม mǎi / mái a particle used to make yes-or-no questions - this one is so common that people sometimes spell it มั้ย
หนังสือ nǎng-sʉ̌ʉ / nǎng-sʉ́ʉ book

No predicting these! Just learn these words as they come.

Vowel length is just weird, plus the vowel-shortener spelling rule

Sometimes vowels lie to you too, and it'll look like it's one length, but it's really another.

เงิน (เงิ็น) ngən money or silver
น้ำ (น้าม) náam water
ต้อง (ต็้อง) dtɔ̂ng have to
เก้า (ก้าว) gâao nine
ได้ (ด้าย) dâai can

This is particularly common when a word "should" have a อ็ on it. There's a spelling rule with อ็: if there's something else already on top, you don't write อ็, even if the vowel is short. This could be a tone mark or a vowel.

Silent letters

Meet this little loopy guy: อ์. This is used to indicate that a consonant is silent.

You see this often in Sanskrit, Pali, and English words where the spelling is preserved but the sounds aren't pronounced in Thai:

โทรศัพท์ (โท-ระ-สับ) too-rá-sàp telephone
ดอลลาร์ (ด็อน-ล่า) dɔn-lâa dollar (Note the unexpected short vowel in dɔn and the unexpected falling tone in lâa! They really wanted to preserve spelling in this word - look at how each Thai letter corresponds to a letter in the English "dollar", despite the pronunciation being different than the letters would suggest.)
เบียร์ (เบีย) biia beer

Rarely, letters are silent and don't have the อ์ on it. This goes for both consonants or the vowels อิ and อุ.

สามารถ (สา-มาด) sǎa-mâat to be able to
ประวัติ (ประ-หวัด) bprà-wàt history
กษัตริย์ (กะ-สัด) gà-sàt king
ศรีราชา (สี-รา-ชา) sǐi-raa-chaa Sriracha hot sauce (yup, English speakers are driving themselves crazy trying to pronounce the s and the r, and Thai people just dropped the r!)

ทร

ทร is a special combination that sometimes, but not always, sounds like s.

ทราบ (ซาบ) sâap a polite word for "to know"
ทรง (ซง) song a shape, or to remember, or to maintain / keep it as it is (this word has like 700 meanings)

ฤ

The name of this letter is รึ rʉ. It can make the sounds รึ rʉ, ริ ri, or เรอ rəə, depending on the word. Note that this letter can be short or long, which affects tone rules as you'd expect.
ภาษาอังกฤษ (อังกริด) paa-sǎa ang-grìt the English language

It has a long version, ฤๅ, which can be read rʉʉ or rii, and there's also ฦ lʉ and ฦๅ lʉʉ. You will literally never see these.

Double consonants

Sometimes, when a word would "normally" have two of the same consonant next to each other, the word is spelled with just one instead.

Take the word มัสมั่น, which is massaman curry (delicious!). It's pronounced like it's มัส-ส-มั่น, but that's a double consonant, so it gets written with only one ส instead.

นิตยสาร (นิต-ตะ-ยะ-สาน) nít-dtà-yá-sǎan magazine
ปัจฉิมบท (ปัด-ฉิม-มะ-บด) bpàt-chǐm-má-bòt epilogue (how fitting!)

ร and รร

ร at the end of a syllable sometimes stands for ออน ɔɔn.

นคร (นะ-คอน) ná-kɔɔn city

รร at the end of a syllable stands for อัน an.

พรรษา (พัน-สา) pan-sǎa rainy season

รร with a consonant after it stands for อั a.

ธรรม (ทำ) tam virtues, especially Buddhist virtues

Words can be different in compounds

Sometimes a word is pronounced differently on its own and in compounds. That last word, ธรรม, is tam on its own, but in compound words, it's pronounced tam-má, like like ธรรมดา tam-má-daa "ordinary". No telling when this happens - just learn the compound words as they come.

ก็

The word ก็ means "also" or "so / therefore" and is pronounced เก้าะ gɔ̂ despite its weird spelling.

Two letters you'll never see

There are two letters of the Thai alphabet that basically got yeeted out: ฃ and ฅ. They look a lot like ข and ค, and they used to function exactly the same. But when Thai typewriters were created, there was no room for all 44 letters, so two had to be left out, and nobody has used these two letters ever since. Words that used to have them are now spelled with regular ol' ข and ค.

Phinthu, the dot that lives in dictionaries

In dictionaries, sometimes the ห from the ห rule and the อ in the four อย words have a little dot placed under them: อฺ. It's called พินทุ pin-tu, which is often spelled "phinthu" in English. It just indicates that the ห and อ aren't regular letters you should pronounce. พินทุ is never used in real life.

Hidden-a clusters without the tone "borrowing"

Remember the normal hidden-a cluster rule? You make the first syllable by adding อะ to the first consonant. Then you "borrow" the consonant class of the first consonant and pretend the second syllable starts with that consonant class.

As it turns out, you only "borrow" the first consonant's class if the second consonant is a sonorant or "sing-y" sound - m n ng r l y w. If the second consonant is anything else, you just read the second syllable as normal.

แสดง, for example, becomes สะ-แดง, where there's no tone borrowing and we read แดง as normal: sà-dɛɛng. We still have to recognize the hidden-a cluster, though; it's not แส-ดง.

Try out these words:

เฉพาะ (ฉะ-เพาะ) chà-pɔ́ specific
แสตมป์ sà-dtɛɛm stamp

Tone borrowers after อํา

Sometimes, if the first syllable of a word has อํา, the second syllable will "borrow" its consonant class from the first consonant of the first syllable.

In the word ดำริ dam-rì, for instance, it's ดำ-ริ, but for this word specifically, we have to know that we pretend the ริ part starts with a mid-class consonant, because the first syllable starts with mid-class ด.

There's no way to tell when this happens. Thankfully, it's super rare. (ดำริ is a word meaning "to think" that's only used by Thai royals. Told you these words were rare.)

กำเนิด (กำ-เหนิด) gam-nə̀ət to be born
สำรวจ (สำ-หรวด) sǎm-rùuat to survey or explore
